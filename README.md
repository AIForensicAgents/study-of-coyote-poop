# study-of-coyote-poop
A field-guide-style, educational repository for studying coyote scat (scat, droppings, feces) — designed for ecologists, students, citizen scientists, and curious naturalists. Professional, practical, and yes, a little cheeky.

---

## Overview
This repository collates observational notes, photographic references, and best-practice guidance for identifying and interpreting coyote feces in the field. It is intended for **non-invasive, observational** work and for informing biological field studies such as diet analysis, population monitoring, and habitat use inference.

What you’ll find here:
- Clear morphological descriptors to help recognize coyote scat.
- Practical clues about diet and habitat gleaned from fecal content.
- Ethics and safety guidance for fieldwork and sample handling.
- An image gallery for reference and training.
- Suggested next steps for research and outreach.

---

## Why it matters
Coyote scat is far more than an unpleasant surprise on a trail — it is a compact, natural data package:
- **Diet and trophic dynamics:** Scat reveals prey species, seasonal shifts in diet, and use of anthropogenic food.
- **Population and territoriality:** Spatial distribution of scat, latrines, and scent-marking sites inform movement and social behavior.
- **Health and disease surveillance:** Parasites and pathogens can be detected in feces (with lab analysis), contributing to wildlife and public health knowledge.
- **Ecosystem monitoring:** Scat records help document species interactions, scavenging, and human-wildlife interfaces.

## Morphology notes
Use these general descriptors as field heuristics. Individual samples vary with age, diet, and environment.

- Shape and form
  - Typically tubular to tapered; variable length (5–20+ cm) and thickness depending on diet.
  - Often segmented or twisted; ends may be tapered or blunt.
- Size
  - Medium-sized among mesocarnivores; thinner than wolf scat, generally thicker than fox scat.
- Color and consistency
  - Fresh scat: dark brown to black, glossy if high in fats (recently fed on mammals).
  - Older/scavenged scat: gray/tan crusting from environmental exposure and insect activity.
- Contents (visible to naked eye)
  - Fur, feathers, bone fragments, seeds, fruit skins, insect exoskeletons, and occasionally anthropogenic items (plastic, foil).
- Odor and moisture
  - Strong odor when fresh; dries and hardens over days.
- Differentiation tips
  - Fox: often smaller and more segmented; coyote scat is generally larger and may contain larger bone fragments.
  - Dog: domestic dog scat can overlap in size; context (location, presence of tracks, repeated latrines) helps differentiate.

Note: Morphology alone does not provide definitive species ID. When accuracy is required, pair morphology with tracks, camera traps, or DNA analysis.

## Habitat / diet clues
Scat contents and placement give insight into habitat use and feeding ecology.

- Diet indicators
  - Hair and bone: mammalian prey (rodents, rabbits, deer carrion).
  - Feathers: avian predation or scavenging.
  - Seeds/fruits: seasonality — fruits (berries, grapes) indicate use of shrublands, riparian zones.
  - Invertebrate remains: insects and beetles — often in arid or seasonal contexts.
  - Anthropogenic items: indicate interaction with human refuse or urban foraging.
- Habitat clues
  - Location on trails, ridgelines, fence posts: territorial marking behavior.
  - Latrines (multiple scats in one spot): territorial boundaries, habitual routes.
  - Proximity to water sources: higher frequency near riparian corridors or urban water features.
  - Elevation and vegetation context: prey availability reflected in scat composition.

Combine scat observations with habitat notes (GPS, vegetation type, nearby anthropogenic features) to build richer inferences.

## Ethics / Safety
Safety and ethics are paramount. This section offers conservative, practical guidance for observers and sample collectors.

- Do not disturb animals
  - Observe from a distance. Do not use lures or bait to attract coyotes.
- Personal protective equipment (PPE)
  - Wear disposable nitrile gloves; eye protection and a surgical or N95 mask are recommended when close to fresh feces.
- Zoonotic risk awareness
  - Coyote scat can carry parasites and pathogens (e.g., Echinococcus, Toxocara, Giardia, bacterial agents). Do not ingest, and avoid touching face while handling samples.
- Sample handling basics (field level)
  - Minimize aerosolization — do not scrape or vacuum feces.
  - Use sealed, leak-proof containers or zip-lock bags for transfer.
  - Label samples with date, time, GPS, and collector.
  - Refrigerate or freeze samples promptly if they will be analyzed.
- Decontamination and disposal
  - Wash hands thoroughly with soap and water after fieldwork.
  - Disinfect tools and surfaces with appropriate disinfectants (e.g., 10% bleach solution where safe and permitted).
  - Dispose of waste following local regulations and institutional biosafety guidelines.
- Permissions and laws
  - Check local wildlife regulations and obtain permits for sample collection or invasive activities. Respect private property.
- Institutional oversight
  - For anything beyond casual observation (e.g., collecting for lab analysis), follow institutional biosafety and animal care protocols and obtain any necessary approvals.

If in doubt, consult a public health officer or institutional biosafety officer before collecting or transporting samples.
